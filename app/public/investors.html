Investment Framework

A Systematic Approach to Site Selection

Every SL|DR site is evaluated against a four-factor composite score. Medical infrastructure receives the highest weight — it is the single variable that determines product eligibility (IL, AL, or MC) and therefore directly governs achievable revenue per unit.

01
Medical Infrastructure
The gating variable. Each care level (IL, AL, MC) requires a minimum medical score (40, 65, and 80 respectively). Without the correct medical ecosystem, the product simply cannot be operated — regardless of demand. Infrastructure gaps can be partially bridged through on-site clinic investment and partnership SLAs.
02
Existing Expat Community
The strongest predictor of absorption velocity. Markets with established international retirement communities have lower CAC, validated product-market fit, and existing social infrastructure. Correlates directly with international media presence and word-of-mouth referral networks.
03
Air Connectivity
Direct routes to source markets — U.S. Northeast, Ontario/Quebec, and Western Europe — determine how quickly a market can be filled and re-filled. Proximity to an international airport also serves as a medical evacuation corridor, partially mitigating infrastructure risk in lower-score markets.
04
Product Differentiation
Each SL|DR community must offer a distinct positioning within the portfolio — preventing cannibalization and enabling targeted channel marketing. Differentiation also drives premium pricing: a Mountain Wellness product in Jarabacoa commands different unit economics than a beachfront resort-adjacent community in Punta Cana.

The Caribbean Has No Institutional Competitor

A systematic review of senior living operators across 20 Caribbean and Latin American markets reveals a landscape with a single defining characteristic: absence. Across Jamaica, Barbados, the Bahamas, the Cayman Islands, the Eastern Caribbean, Aruba, Curaçao, Turks & Caicos, and Trinidad — collectively hosting an estimated 200,000+ international retirees — not one institutional-grade Independent Living community exists. One professional regional operator (Insignia Senior Living) serves the urban AL/MC segment in Puerto Rico and Panama City. One small CCRC operates in San José, Costa Rica — inland, not coastal. The Caribbean beach corridor senior living market is, in its entirety, unbuilt.

Why the Gap Persists

01

Regulatory Complexity

Operating senior living in a foreign jurisdiction requires navigating local healthcare licensing, labor law, real estate title, and repatriation protocols — simultaneously. U.S. operators lack the local expertise. Local developers lack the operational know-how. SL|DR bridges both.

02

Capital Requirements

Institutional-grade communities require $15M–$40M in development capital — well above the threshold of local developers, and below the minimum ticket size for most U.S. institutional operators eyeing international markets. SL|DR is sized precisely for this middle window.

03

First-Mover Moat

In senior living, brand trust is earned through word-of-mouth within tightly networked expat communities. The operator that builds first accumulates referral equity that is structurally unavailable to second entrants — especially in markets as socially cohesive as Sosúa, Las Terrenas, and the VISTACANA resident community.

The Competitive Conclusion

Across 20 markets and an estimated 300,000+ qualified international retirees living in the Caribbean basin, the institutional senior living addressable market is served by approximately 660 beds total — 600 at Insignia Puerto Rico (urban AL/MC) and 61 at the Escazú CCRC (urban Costa Rica). That is one bed per 450 qualified residents.

The Dominican Republic senior living market in 2026 is structurally analogous to the Costa Rica vacation rental market in 2005 or the Mexico retirement villa market in 2010: a large, qualified demand base, zero institutional supply, and an early-mover window that closes progressively as capital and operators identify the opportunity. SL|DR's Phase 1 asset (VISTACANA) is already under development — not planning, not proposed, but building. That timeline advantage compounds: by the time a second institutional operator identifies the market, underwrites a deal, raises capital, and breaks ground, SL|DR will have 3–4 years of operating history, a proven NOI track record, and a referral network covering every English-speaking expat community on the island.

The International Resident Base

The Dominican Republic hosts one of the Caribbean's largest established international resident communities. Official State Department registration captures approximately 14,600 U.S. citizens, but total estimates including dual citizens, permanent long-term residents, and retirees on tourist visa extensions reach 200,000–250,000. The country ranks among the top five globally for VA Foreign Medical Program overseas claims — a direct proxy for veteran retirement concentration — alongside Panama, Costa Rica, the Philippines, and Germany. Law 171-07 (Pensionado Visa) requires only $1,500/month in verified pension income, making DR accessible to nearly all U.S. military retirees, VA disability recipients (100% rating: $3,831/mo in 2025), and Social Security beneficiaries.

The 548,000+ Dominicans abroad aged 55 — the generation that emigrated to New York, New Jersey, and Florida in the 1970s–1990s — are reaching peak retirement age simultaneously. Many maintain property in the Dominican Republic, speak Spanish at home, and have aging parents already on the island. This cohort does not need to be convinced to return — they need a product that makes return viable. SL|DR's Santo Domingo and Santiago communities are designed explicitly for this segment: urban, family-proximate, institutionally-supported, and culturally resonant. The adult-child decision-maker (age 40–55, based in the U.S.) is the primary marketing target — not the future resident.

The Demand Case

Three independent demand forces converge on a single conclusion: the Dominican Republic is structurally undersupplied in senior living. The domestic 65+ population will nearly double by 2050. The international expat community of 300,000–400,000 residents has no institutional care option. And 548,000 diaspora Dominicans aged 55+ are approaching retirement with strong return motivation and U.S.-calibrated income. Against this backdrop, SL|DR is the only institutional operator building toward this market at scale. First-mover advantage in a market this large, with this many converging demographic tailwinds, is not a temporary edge — it is a structural moat.
